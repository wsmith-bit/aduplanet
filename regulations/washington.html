Washington ADU Regulations — 2025 Statewide Guide

What Washington’s 2023–2025 laws mean for your backyard home: two ADUs per lot, at least 1,000 sf allowed, simpler parking, and no owner-occupancy rules.

TL;DR: In Washington’s GMA urban growth areas, cities and counties must allow two ADUs per lot, permit at least 1,000 sf per ADU, and cannot require owner-occupancy. Near major transit (½-mile), no on-site parking can be required. Impact fees are capped at ≤ 50% of the principal home’s fees. Always confirm local implementation timelines.

The statewide rules at a glance

Core statewide requirements

How many?
Two ADUs per residential lot in GMA urban growth areas (attached, detached, or combo). Source
Minimum size allowed
At least 1,000 sf must be allowed; locals may allow more. Source
Owner-occupancy
Not required for principal or ADU (exception may apply for STRs). Source
Parking
No on-site parking may be required within ½ mile of major transit; elsewhere, limits apply by lot size. Source
Impact fees
Capped at ≤ 50% of the principal unit’s fees. Source
Setbacks/height/aesthetics
Must be no more restrictive than for the principal unit (no ADU-only caps). Source

How to permit an ADU (statewide flow)

  1. Confirm eligibility. You’re inside a GMA urban growth area, and your lot supports a principal dwelling (existing or proposed).
  2. Pick your ADU path. Attached ADU, detached ADU (DADU), or convert existing structure (e.g., garage or outbuilding).
  3. Run the code checklist. Plan for ≥1,000 sf; apply the same height/setbacks as the principal; check parking rules based on transit proximity.
  4. Prepare submittals. Site plan, floor plans, elevations, energy/MEP forms; many cities publish ADU checklists. Commerce ADU permit checklist
  5. Apply online & pay fees. Impact fees are ≤50% of a new house. Use your local permit portal (directory: city & county websites).
  6. Build & inspect. Typical inspections: foundation, framing, rough-in MEP, insulation/energy, final. {{verify locally}}

Shortcuts: Many cities offer pre-approved DADU plans (e.g., Seattle ADUniverse, Renton, Kirkland). Availability varies.

What this changes in practice

  • Two ADUs now standard in urban growth areas → more flexibility for multigenerational use and rental income.
  • Parity standards (height/setbacks/design) remove many ADU-only constraints that used to slow projects.
  • Lower carrying costs via impact-fee cap (≤50% of main home).
  • Parking can’t be required near major transit; elsewhere there are limits (check local lot-size rules). {{verify locally}}

Always cross-check your city/county’s implementation timeline; state law preempts after periodic update windows. Commerce guidance

Washington ADU FAQ (statewide)

Can my city still require owner-occupancy?

No. State law prohibits owner-occupancy requirements for ADUs or the principal unit. An exception may apply where the property is used for short-term rentals. See Commerce.

Is 1,000 sf the maximum size?

No—1,000 sf is the minimum that must be allowed. Many jurisdictions allow more (e.g., up to 1,200 sf). Check your local code. MRSC overview.

Do I need to add parking?

If your ADU is within ½-mile of a major transit stop, your city/county cannot require on-site parking for the ADU. Outside that radius, limited requirements may apply based on lot size. {{verify locally}}

What are the setbacks and height limits?

ADUs must be regulated no more strictly than the principal house for setbacks, lot coverage, and height. Specific numbers (e.g., 5-foot side setbacks, 30-foot height, etc.) are local. {{verify locally}}

Can I sell my ADU separately?

Yes. State law allows ADUs to be sold separately (commonly via condo or unit-lot subdivision) where local procedures exist. See your jurisdiction’s subdivision/condo process and Commerce guidance.

Where do I start with permits?

Use your city/county permit portal. Many publish ADU checklists and pre-approved plan sets. Directory: city & county websites. State checklist: ADU building permit checklist.
